## **II. The Magian Episteme: The Structure of the Target**

To understand why the *DƒÅmdƒÅd Nask* was a target for both translation and destruction, one must first comprehend the unique epistemological framework it represented. The Magian intellectual tradition was not a monolithic superstition but a highly structured system of knowledge management that categorized reality through the lens of "Universal Correspondence." This system posited a universe that was morally legible, where the physical characteristics of an object were direct signatures of its spiritual allegiance and function.

### **A. The DƒÅmdƒÅd Nask and the Hadha-manthric Corpus**

The Sasanian Avesta, as recalled by the later tradition, was divided into three functional categories: the *GƒÅhƒÅnƒ´g* (Gathic/Liturgical), the *DƒÅdƒ´g* (Legal), and the *Hadha-manthric* (Scientific/Mixed). The *DƒÅmdƒÅd Nask* ("The Nask of the Creation of Creatures") belonged to this third category. It was, in effect, the "Scientific Avesta," an encyclopedic volume that functioned as a "Universal Taxonomy of Correspondence".  
While the liturgical texts (*Manthra*) were preserved orally to maintain the phonological sanctity of the sacred word‚Äîa practice that ensured their survival even after the material destruction of the archives‚Äîthe *Hadha-manthric* texts contained "low-constraint" taxonomic data that necessitated a written tradition. Lists of plant species, astronomical tables of planetary motion, medical recipes involving specific ratios of ingredients, and geographical surveys of the *Karshvars* (climes) could not be trusted to the fluid memory of oral recitation. These were likely recorded on perishable media like parchment or ox-hides, making them uniquely vulnerable to physical destruction.  
The contents of the *DƒÅmdƒÅd Nask*, partially reconstructed in the 9th-century *Bundahi≈°n* and the *Selections of ZƒÅdspram*, reveal a sophisticated biological and cosmological system. This classification extended to botany, where plants were categorized by their capacity to heal or poison, corresponding to their celestial patrons. This was a "Science of Correspondences" where the physical world (*gƒìtƒ´g*) was the visible manifestation of a spiritual cause (*mƒìn≈çg*).

The metaphor of the "kernel" and the "shell" is central to understanding the Magian view of the catastrophe. The *DƒÅmdƒÅd Nask* represented the "kernel"‚Äîthe explanatory scientific key (Zand) that unlocked the meaning of the rituals. The liturgy and the external laws represented the "shell."

## **III. The Academy and the East: The Philosophical Motive**

The destruction of Persepolis was not an isolated military incident but the culmination of a centuries-long intellectual engagement between the Greek Academy and the Magian priesthood. The "Science of Correspondences" did not arrive in Athens by accident; it was imported through specific vectors, primarily Eudoxus of Cnidus, and deeply influenced the philosophical maturation of Plato and Aristotle. The Greek Academy's attitude toward the East was characterized by a profound ambivalence: a reverence for the "ancient" wisdom of Zoroaster and a simultaneous disdain for the "corrupt" practices of the contemporary Magi.

### **A. Eudoxus of Cnidus: The Architect of Transmission**

Eudoxus of Cnidus (c. 390‚Äì337 BCE), a mathematician, astronomer, and acting head of the Academy during Plato's absences, serves as the pivotal link in this transmission. Pliny the Elder explicitly records that Eudoxus declared Zoroaster to be the "most useful" (*utilissimus*) of philosophers and dated him to 6,000 years before Plato.  
This endorsement is significant. It suggests that Eudoxus viewed Zoroastrianism not merely as a foreign religion but as a pragmatic system of knowledge‚Äîa "useful" philosophy. Eudoxus‚Äôs own work in astronomy, specifically the theory of concentric spheres, bears a structural resemblance to the Magian cosmology of the *DƒÅmdƒÅd Nask*, which described the universe as a series of concentric protective spheres (or *Karshvars*).  
Eudoxus traveled extensively in Egypt and likely had contact with Magian priests operating within the Persian satrapies. His validation of Zoroaster legitimised the study of Oriental wisdom within the Academy, creating an intellectual climate where the "Magian Science" was seen as a reservoir of ancient, pristine truth that had been lost to the Greeks. This pre-disposed the Academy to view the Persian archives not as barbarian ramblings, but as a repository of "Source Code" for the cosmos. It was Eudoxus who likely first introduced the specific details of the *mƒìn≈çg*/*gƒìtƒ´g* distinction to Plato, framing it in the geometric language of Forms and Particulars.

### **B. Plato‚Äôs Bricolage and the "Myth of Er"**

The influence of this Magian data on Plato is evident in his later period, where he engages in what can be termed "mythic bricolage"‚Äîthe restructuring of Greek dialectic using Magian ontological bricks. The most contentious piece of evidence for this is the accusation by the Epicurean Colotes of Lampsacus (3rd century BCE) that Plato plagiarized his "Myth of Er" (Republic X) from a Zoroastrian text titled *On Nature*.  
The "Myth of Er" describes a journey of the soul through a concentric cosmos, involving judgment, planetary spheres, and the choice of a next life. This narrative structure is strikingly isomorphic to the *Book of Arda Viraf* and the cosmography of the *DƒÅmdƒÅd Nask*. The Zoroastrian *Arda Viraf* travels through Heaven, Hell, and *Hamistagan* (a Purgatory or "Place of the Mixed"), guided by Sraosha and Atar, just as Er is guided through the planetary whorls. The "Spindle of Necessity" in Plato corresponds to the "Chinvat Bridge" and the planetary spheres traversed by the soul in the *Hadha-manthric* texts.  
The "Science of Correspondences" hypothesis argues that Plato did not "invent" these myths but adapted translated summaries of the *Hadha-manthric* texts provided by Eudoxus or Hermodorus. Plato‚Äôs *Timaeus*, with its geometric atomism and the role of the Demiurge, also reflects a systematization of the Magian *mƒìn≈çg*/*gƒìtƒ´g* dualism. The Demiurge (Divine Craftsman) looks to the eternal Forms (*mƒìn≈çg*) to fashion the material world (*gƒìtƒ´g*) within the Receptacle (*chora*). Plato represents an attempt to "rescue" this ancient science of vertical causality before the "Epistemological Fracture" became total. He tried to translate the intuitive "perception" of the Golden Age into the "reason" of the Iron Age.

### **C. Aristotle: The Distinction Between Ancient Wisdom and Modern Magic**

Aristotle‚Äôs relationship with the Magi is complex and marks the shift from preservation to appropriation. While Plato and Eudoxus looked to Zoroaster with reverence as an ancient sage, Aristotle introduced a critical distinction between the "ancient" Zoroaster and the "contemporary" Magi.  
In his lost dialogue *On Philosophy* (*Peri Philosophias*) and the treatise *Magikos*, Aristotle acknowledges the Magi as being "more ancient than the Egyptians" and credits them with the doctrine of two principles: Oromasdes (Zeus/Good) and Areimanius (Hades/Evil). He recognizes their philosophical pedigree. However, Aristotle was also a rigorous naturalist who rejected the independent existence of Platonic Forms (*mƒìn≈çg*). For Aristotle, the "form" was immanent in the matter.  
The "Science of Correspondences," which relied on the separation of spiritual cause and natural effect, was philosophically incompatible with Aristotle‚Äôs physics. Yet, the *data* contained in the Magian books‚Äîthe observations of stars, plants, and animals‚Äîwas invaluable. Aristotle faced a dilemma: the Persian archives contained the empirical data he craved for his natural histories (biology, meteorology, astronomy), but it was encoded in a theological framework he rejected.  
This creates the motive for the "Alexandrian solution": extract the data (translation) and eliminate the framework (destruction). Aristotle advised Alexander to treat the Greeks as a leader and the barbarians as a master (treating them like plants or animals) , a sentiment that aligns with the "strip-mining" of their intellectual resources. The *DƒÅmdƒÅd Nask* was to be harvested for its *facts*, stripped of its *correspondences*. Aristotle‚Äôs interest was in the *classification* of the animals (as seen in his *History of Animals*), not in their function as *g≈çspand* or *xrafstra* in a cosmic war.

## **IV. The Alexandrian Event: The Translation and the Arson**

The actual mechanism of this "epistemicide" is detailed in Zoroastrian historiography, which offers a narrative radically different from the Greek accounts of accidental fire. The burning of Persepolis was not merely an act of arson; it was the final stage of a data extraction process.

The *Dƒìnkard* (Book 4 and Book 3, Chapter 420\) and the *Book of Arda Viraf* provide a specific account of Alexander's actions regarding the archives. They state:

"That which was in the fortress of documents came to be burnt; and that in the treasury... was translated by him even into the Greek language, as information which was connected \[with the sciences\]...".  
This "Translation Order" is the smoking gun of the hypothesis. Alexander did not indiscriminately burn everything. The texts in the treasury‚Äîlikely the "working copies" or the specific scientific Nasks valued by court astronomers and physicians‚Äîwere seized and translated. The target was the *Hadha-manthric* corpus: the *DƒÅmdƒÅd Nask* (Cosmogony/Natural History), the *Ti≈°tar Ya≈°t* (Astronomy), and the medical treatises. The *DƒÅmdƒÅd Nask*, as the encyclopedia of creation, was the primary victim of this policy. It contained the "Source Code" of Magian science. By translating it, the Greeks acquired:

Once the "useful" information (the *utilissimus* part praised by Eudoxus) was extracted into Greek, the original ox-hides‚Äîwritten in Avestan/Aramaic scripts and inextricably linked to the "corrupt" dualistic theology‚Äîwere burned. ### **C. The Role of Hermippus and the Library**

The hypothesis is further supported by the figure of Hermippus of Smyrna (3rd century BCE). Pliny records that Hermippus, a scholar at the Library of Alexandria, "explained the contents of two million verses composed by Zoroaster" and compiled an index of them.  
Where did Hermippus get access to "two million verses" of Zoroaster in Alexandria, barely a century after Alexander? The answer, under this hypothesis, is that these were the very translations ordered by Alexander from the Persepolis archives. The Library of Alexandria became the new repository for the "sanitized" Magian science. The *DƒÅmdƒÅd Nask* did not vanish; it metamorphosed into the Greek scientific corpus, leaving the Persian priesthood with only the memory of the texts and the oral liturgy. ## **V. Philosophical Motivation: Corruption vs. Purity**

Why was destruction necessary? Why not simply keep the originals? The answer lies in the philosophical battle over the nature of "true" science and the Aristotelian drive to rationalize the cosmos.

### **A. The "True" Science vs. Contemporary Corruption**

By the 4th century BCE, Greek intellectuals like Plato and Aristotle drew a sharp line between the "ancient" wisdom of Zoroaster (which they idealized) and the practices of the contemporary Magi (which they often viewed as *goetia* or sorcery).

* **The Ancient Zoroaster:** Viewed as a proto-philosopher, a star-gazer, and a master of natural science. Eudoxus and Aristotle praised this figure as the discoverer of wisdom (*sophia*).  
* **The Contemporary Magi:** Seen by some Greeks, particularly the more rationalist factions, as engaging in "begging-priest" rituals, incantations, and "magic" in the pejorative sense. The term *goetia* was used to describe low magic, distinct from the high *mageia* or *theurgia* attributed to Zoroaster.

The destruction of the Avestan archives can be interpreted as a "Reformation" by fire. By destroying the contemporary texts‚Äîwhich bound the scientific data to a "corrupt" priestly hierarchy and ritual system‚ÄîAlexander and his advisors may have believed they were liberating the "true" ancient science of Zoroaster. They were rescuing the *essence* (the logical data) from the *accident* (the Persian cult). This mirrors the Platonic critique of writing in the *Phaedrus*, where the written word is seen as a dead image of living truth‚Äîperhaps the Greeks felt justified in destroying the "dead letter" of the Persian books to appropriate the "living spirit" of the science.

### **B. Aristotle‚Äôs Rationalizing Project**

Aristotle‚Äôs philosophy required a stable, eternal world where causes were immanent. The Magian worldview, with its battles between cosmic spirits, its creation from a spiritual *mƒìn≈çg*, and its reliance on "correspondences," was fundamentally incompatible with Aristotelian naturalism. The *DƒÅmdƒÅd Nask* described a universe where a "Sole-Created Ox" was the progenitor of all animals and where disease was an invasion by a demon (*Druj*).  
However, Aristotle was also a pragmatist. He needed data. The "Translation Order" reflects an Aristotelian approach to knowledge: **collection, categorization, and rationalization**. The *DƒÅmdƒÅd Nask* was a treasure trove of raw data. The goal was to assimilate this data into the new, universal Greek *episteme* while erasing the "barbarian" authority that produced it.  
This mirrors the "Letter of Tansar" tradition where Alexander is advised to kill the Persian elites (the carriers of the tradition) but is eventually told to divide them to prevent unity. Destroying the central archive at Persepolis and translating the science effectively "divided" the knowledge, breaking the monopoly of the Magian priesthood and preventing the resurrection of their theocratic power. The burning was a technology of control, ensuring that the source of the knowledge would be forgotten, leaving only the Greek derivative as the authoritative text.

## **VI. The Aftermath: Reconstruction and the Lost Word**

The consequences of this "Archival Rupture" were profound and permanent, shaping the trajectory of both Western science and Eastern religion. It created two divergent streams of knowledge: a Western science bereft of spiritual causality and an Eastern religion bereft of its scientific foundation.

In contrast, Iran entered a "dark age" of literacy. The *Dƒìnkard* laments that the transmission of the Avesta became fragmented and oral. The "scientific" Nasks were lost. ### **B. The Arabic Tradition: The "Secret of Secrets"**

The memory of this event and the relationship between Aristotle and Alexander survived in the Islamic world, mutated into the legend of the *Sirr al-Asrar* (Secret of Secrets). This pseudo-Aristotelian text, widely popular in the Middle Ages, purports to be a letter from Aristotle to Alexander advising him on statecraft and "secret" knowledge.  
While apocryphal, the *Sirr al-Asrar* preserves the *idea* of Aristotle as the guiding hand behind Alexander‚Äôs policies in Persia. In some versions, Aristotle advises Alexander not to kill the Persian elites but to govern them; in others, he provides esoteric knowledge about the "nature" of the lands. The text‚Äôs focus on physiognomy, medicine, and astrology‚Äîtopics covered in the *DƒÅmdƒÅd Nask*‚Äîsuggests a lingering association between Aristotelian advice and Magian science.

### **C. Swedenborg and the "Ancient Word"**

The long-term esoteric legacy of this rupture is articulated by Emanuel Swedenborg in the 18th century.
