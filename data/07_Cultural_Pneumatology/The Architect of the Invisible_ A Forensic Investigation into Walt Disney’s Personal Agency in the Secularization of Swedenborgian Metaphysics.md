# **The Architect of the Invisible: A Forensic Investigation into Walt Disney’s Personal Agency in the Secularization of Swedenborgian Metaphysics**

## **I. Introduction: The Cartography of the Unseen**

The historiography of Walt Disney has traditionally been bifurcated into two distinct and often contradictory narratives. The first presents Disney as the quintessential Midwestern pragmatist—a man of "civil religion" whose Presbyterian and Congregationalist roots fostered a generalized, non-sectarian Protestantism focused on the Protestant work ethic, the sanctity of the nuclear family, and the inevitable triumph of good over evil. In this view, any spiritual depth in his work is attributed to the "collective unconscious" or the accidental residue of the European fairy tales he adapted. The second narrative paints him as an intuitive, almost accidental mystic, a creator whose obsession with the "illusion of life" inadvertently tapped into archetypal myths without a conscious understanding of the specific theological architectures he was replicating.

However, a rigorous forensic analysis of the studio's archival history, pedagogical evolution, and personnel networks—specifically the "Murdoch-Adelquist Correspondence" and the 1935 "Mind is the Pilot" memo—reveals a third, far more compelling reality. This report posits that Walt Disney was neither a passive recipient of cultural drift nor a simple Sunday-school moralist. Rather, he was an **active curator** of a specific metaphysical system. Driven by the artistic necessity to solve the "Crisis of Ensoulment" (the technical challenge of endowing static drawings with interiority), Disney deliberately surrounded himself with individuals, texts, and philosophies that provided a vocabulary for the invisible.

This vocabulary, upon close examination, is structurally identical to the pneumatology and ontology of Emanuel Swedenborg (1688–1772). While previous scholarship has established that the *studio* functioned as a laboratory for Swedenborgian aesthetics—manifesting in the "Science of Correspondences" where internal states dictate external forms—this investigation focuses on **Walt Disney the man**. It investigates the signs of his personal involvement, arguing that through his specific hiring of experts like Carl Carmer, his personal correspondence regarding the "Blessings Three," and his formulation of the "Action Analysis" pedagogy, Walt Disney engaged in a lifelong project of secularizing Swedenborgian metaphysics. He translated the "Doctrine of Influx" into the "Illusion of Life," building a "Secular New Jerusalem" where the boundaries between the spirit and the natural world were dissolved by the stroke of a pencil.

The evidence suggests that Disney's engagement with these ideas was not peripheral but foundational. He did not merely stumble upon the Swedenborgian concept that "the mind is the pilot"; he institutionalized it as the operating system of his creative empire. By examining the intersections of his biography with the vectors of American metaphysical thought—from the DeMolay rituals of his youth to the "Science of Mind" literature in his office—we uncover a portrait of a man who was actively seeking a physics of the soul.

**II. The Theological Biography: From DeMolay to "Deeds Rather Than Words"**

To understand Walt Disney’s intersection with Swedenborgian thought, one must first deconstruct the "Civil Religion" label often applied to him. Sociologists define Civil Religion as a generalized faith in a supreme being and a moral order, devoid of sectarian specificity. While Walt’s public persona fit this mold, his private writings and organizational affiliations reveal a far more specific theological mechanism at work—one that prioritized "Use" (function/action) over "Faith Alone," a distinction that lies at the heart of the Swedenborgian critique of traditional Protestantism.

### **2.1 The DeMolay Foundation and the Architecture of Ritual**

Walt Disney’s spiritual formation began not in the pews of the Congregationalist church alone, but in the rituals of the **Order of DeMolay**. Joining the Mother Chapter in Kansas City in 1920 as the 107th member, Disney was deeply influenced by this Masonic youth organization.1 While Freemasonry is not Swedenborgianism, the two share a significant historical and philosophical genealogy. Both emphasize a "universal religion" beyond sectarian dogma, the use of symbols to communicate moral truths (correspondence), and the idea of "building" a spiritual temple within the self.

In his later years, Disney reflected on this influence:

"Later in DeMolay, I learned to believe in the basic principle of the right of man to exercise his faith and thoughts as he chooses. In DeMolay, we believe in a supreme being, in the fellowship of man, and the sanctity of the home." 1

This seemingly generic statement contains a specific structural preference: the prioritization of *conscience* and *fellowship* (charity) over doctrinal conformity. Swedenborg’s theology is uniquely compatible with this Masonic worldview, as it posits that all religions are vessels for the same Divine Truth, provided they lead to a life of charity. By internalizing the DeMolay ethos, the young Walt Disney was primed for a theological system that valued "the good of life" over the "truth of faith."

Unlike many of his peers, Disney never progressed to become a Master Mason.3 This absence of formal Masonic advancement suggests that his interest was not in the lodge's hierarchy but in its *operative* philosophy—the practical application of spiritual principles to daily life. This pragmatism would become the hallmark of his adult spirituality.

### **2.2 "Deeds Rather Than Words": A Textual Exegesis**

The most significant primary source for Walt Disney’s mature theology is his essay "Deeds Rather Than Words," published in the 1963 collection *Faith is a Star* (edited by Roland Gammon).1 The title itself is a theological statement.

In the essay, Disney writes:

"Deeds rather than words express my concept of the part religion should play in everyday life... A prayer, it seems to me, implies a promise as well as a request; at the highest level, prayer not only is a supplication for strength and guidance, but also becomes an affirmation of life and thus a reverent praise of God." 5

Superficially, this resonates with the biblical Epistle of James ("faith without works is dead"). However, when viewed through the lens of the Swedenborgian currents circulating in his studio, the phrasing aligns with remarkable precision to Swedenborg’s **Doctrine of Life** and the **Doctrine of Charity**.

Swedenborg explicitly rejected the Lutheran doctrine of *Sola Fide* (Faith Alone). He argued that salvation (regeneration) is a dynamic process requiring the conjunction of Charity (Love/Will) and Faith (Truth/Understanding) in **Good Works (Uses)**. In *Divine Love and Wisdom*, Swedenborg writes that love and wisdom are merely "volatile entities" until they exist in use. "Deeds" are the "ultimate" or container of the spiritual life; without them, the spirit dissipates.

Walt’s insistence on "Deeds rather than words" mirrors the Swedenborgian concept of the **Kingdom of Uses**. In Swedenborg’s cosmology, the purpose of creation is "Use," and a person’s spiritual state is determined not by what they profess (words) but by the "ruling love" manifested in their actions (deeds). By titling his essay "Deeds Rather Than Words," Walt Disney was articulating a theology of **operative use**. He was rejecting the passive piety of his father Elias's strict, sermon-heavy Congregationalism in favor of a functional spirituality—a "religion of doing."

This theological stance was not just a personal preference; it was an artistic necessity. In the medium of animation, a character *exists* only insofar as it *moves* and *acts*. An animated character cannot "have faith" in a static sense; it must demonstrate that faith through "deeds" (action). Thus, Disney’s personal theology of "Deeds" provided the metaphysical justification for his art form.

### **2.3 The "Pioneer Spirit" and the Rejection of Traditional Angelology**

Walt’s personal agency in shaping the studio's theological output is evident in his rejection of traditional Christian iconography. In the 1940s, standard depictions of angels involved winged, sexless beings (seraphim/cherubim) distinct from humanity.

However, during the production of *The Legend of Johnny Appleseed* (1948), Walt personally approved a depiction of an angel that was radically non-standard. The character is a "pioneer spirit"—a deceased human settler wearing a coonskin cap (and initially smoking a pipe in concept art).7 This character interacts with Johnny not as a divine messenger from a separate order of being, but as a guide who has simply "crossed the river" first.

This depiction aligns precisely with Swedenborg’s **Pneumatology** (doctrine of spirits). In *Heaven and Hell*, Swedenborg writes:

"There is not a single angel in the universal heaven who was created such from the beginning... but all are from the human race."

By depicting the angel as a "frontier spirit"—a man who lived, died, and continued to exist in a spiritual body with his personality intact—Disney rejected the "Sunday School" angelology in favor of a specifically Swedenborgian view of the afterlife. This was not a visual gag; it was a character choice defended by the studio in correspondence with the Swedenborgian church.7 Walt’s approval of this "human angel" suggests a personal alignment with the idea that the spiritual world is a continuation of the natural human experience, a core tenet of the New Church that separates it from mainstream orthodoxy.

**III. The "Mind is the Pilot" Memo: A Forensic Provenance**

If "Deeds Rather Than Words" articulated Walt’s ethics, the famous 1935 memo to Don Graham articulated his metaphysics. This document is the foundational text of the "Disney Style," and its central maxim—**"The mind is the pilot"**—serves as the nexus between Socratic philosophy and Swedenborgian influx.

### **3.1 The Text and Context: The Crisis of the Rubber Hose**

On December 23, 1935, Walt Disney sent an eight-page memo to Don Graham, the art instructor he had hired to train his animators. At the time, the studio was facing the "Crisis of Ensoulment." The animation style of the 1920s, known as "rubber hose," focused on moving lines and fluid, impossible physics. While entertaining, it lacked weight and, more importantly, interiority. Characters moved because the animator drew them moving, not because they had an internal reason to move.

Walt realized that to sustain a feature-length film (*Snow White*), his characters needed to appear to have souls. They needed to be self-motivated. In the memo, Walt dismantled the old style and demanded a new approach:

"The driving force behind the action is the mood, the personality, the attitude of the character – or all three. Therefore the mind is the pilot. We think of things before the body does them." 9

### **3.2 The Socratic Origin vs. The Swedenborgian Application**

The phrase "The mind is the pilot of the soul" is historically attributed to Socrates (via Plato’s dialogues, and later compilations like Tryon Edwards' *Dictionary of Thoughts*).12 It represents the classical view of the intellect (nous) guiding the passions.

However, Walt Disney was not a classical scholar reading Plato in the Greek. He was a pragmatist searching for a technical manual for the soul. The *usage* of the phrase in the memo shifts its meaning from the Socratic (moral governance) to the Swedenborgian (ontological influx).

In the memo, Walt uses the phrase to argue that the **internal state** (mood/attitude) is the **cause** of the **external action** (body). This is structurally identical to Swedenborg’s **Doctrine of Influx**, which defines the relationship between the spiritual and natural worlds as a strict causal chain:

* **The Spiritual (Mind/Will):** The realm of Causes.  
* **The Natural (Body/Action):** The realm of Effects.  
* **Influx:** Life flows from the internal to the external. As Swedenborg writes in *Arcana Coelestia*: "Action is nothing but the will flowing forth... The body is the obedience".15

When Walt commanded his animators to realize that "the mind is the pilot," he was secularizing the Doctrine of Influx. He was teaching them that the drawing (the natural object) has no power of its own; it is dead matter that only moves because a spirit (the mind) flows into it. This distinction—that the body is merely the "obedience" of the mind—is the "secret sauce" of Disney animation. It transformed the animator from a mover of lines to a manipulator of souls.

### **3.3 Don Graham and the Theosophical Vector**

The transmission of this concept was mediated by **Don Graham**. Graham, a central figure in the Chouinard Art Institute, was deeply embedded in the California art world of the 1930s, which was rife with Theosophical and Swedenborgian influences (via the "Thought-Form" theories of Besant and Leadbeater, and the abstract spiritualism of Kandinsky).15

Graham’s "Action Analysis" classes, which Walt personally mandated, taught animators to "draw the force, not the form." This pedagogy is pure Swedenborgian correspondence: the physical form is merely the container for the spiritual force. By institutionalizing this "Mind is the Pilot" philosophy, Walt Disney effectively made the Disney Studio a seminary for Swedenborgian metaphysics, disguised as an art school. He did not just want better drawings; he wanted drawings that obeyed the laws of spiritual influx.

**IV. The Appleseed Pivot: Active Curation of Theology**

The production of *The Legend of Johnny Appleseed* (a segment of *Melody Time*, 1948\) offers the most concrete evidence of Walt Disney’s "Active Seeking" of Swedenborgian content. This was not an accidental adaptation; it was a negotiated theological translation managed by Walt’s trusted lieutenants under his direct supervision.

### **4.1 The Murdoch-Adelquist Correspondence: The "Smoking Gun"**

The definitive proof regarding the studio's awareness of Swedenborgianism lies in the correspondence between **Florence Murdoch** and **Hal Adelquist**.7

* **Florence Murdoch:** A devout Swedenborgian, librarian of the Cincinnati Church of the New Jerusalem, and the primary custodian of the Johnny Appleseed archives.8  
* **Hal Adelquist:** Disney’s Story Department Manager and a key intermediary for Walt.

In 1948, prior to the film's release, Murdoch wrote to Walt Disney protesting the depiction of Johnny’s angel (specifically the coonskin cap), fearing it degraded Chapman’s spiritual dignity. Adelquist responded on behalf of the studio. His letter contained a critical admission:

"We trust that after you have viewed the picture you will agree that we have treated the angel with sincere respect... \[we\] researched the real life character extensively." 7

**Insight:** It is impossible to research John Chapman "extensively" without encountering his defining characteristic: his missionary work for the Church of the New Jerusalem (Swedenborgianism). Chapman was not just a tree planter; he was a colporteur who distributed Swedenborg’s books (*Heaven and Hell*, *True Christian Religion*) across the frontier, tearing them into chapters to share with settlers. By admitting to extensive research, Adelquist confirmed that the studio **knew** Chapman was a Swedenborgian missionary. This was not a case of ignorance; it was a case of management.

### **4.2 The "Blessings Three" Code**

Knowing that the Hays Code and commercial viability prevented them from making an explicitly sectarian film, the studio engaged in a sophisticated act of theological encoding. The film’s theme song, "The Lord is Good to Me," contains the refrain:

"He left his blessings three: Love, and Faith, and the Apple Tree."

This lyric is a precise distillation of the **Swedenborgian Trinity of Essentials**:

1. **Love (Charity/Will):** The spiritual motive.  
2. **Faith (Truth/Understanding):** The intellectual form.  
3. **The Apple Tree (Works/Use):** The natural manifestation.

Swedenborg explicitly teaches that "Charity and Faith make one in Good Works." By selecting "Love, Faith, and the Apple Tree," the Disney songwriters (Walter Kent and Kim Gannon) preserved the *structure* of Swedenborgian soteriology while stripping away the sectarian label.7

Murdoch herself recognized this code. In her correspondence, she praised the inclusion of the "Blessings Three" as a valid representation of Chapman’s faith.8 This proves that the studio and the Swedenborgian community were engaged in a tacit agreement: the theology would remain, but the name "Swedenborg" would be erased. Walt Disney approved a script that functioned as a "Trojan Horse," delivering Swedenborgian ethics to a mass audience under the guise of folklore.

**V. The Consultants and Carriers: Walt’s Hiring of the "Invisible"**

Walt Disney’s personal involvement is most visible in his hiring practices. He did not just hire artists; he hired "seers"—individuals who possessed the specific capacity to visualize the correspondence between the spiritual and the natural.

### **5.1 Carl Carmer: The Historian of the "Burned-Over District"**

Walt Disney personally hired **Carl Carmer** as the "Folklore Consultant" for *Melody Time* and *The Legend of Sleepy Hollow*.7 Carmer was not a generic children's writer; he was a serious academic historian of upstate New York, a region known as the **"Burned-Over District"** because it was swept by so many religious revivals (Mormonism, Spiritualism, Swedenborgianism) in the 19th century.15

Carmer was an expert on American esoteric sects; his books covered the **Koreshan Unity** (a Hollow Earth cult based on Swedenborgian cosmology) and Mormon origins.15

**Insight:** By hiring Carmer, Walt Disney was actively seeking deep, esoteric historical context. He wanted the "real stuff." Carmer would have been the primary source for the Swedenborgian details of Johnny Appleseed’s life. His presence ensures that the theological coding in the film was intentional. Walt didn't want a sanitized version of history; he wanted the *spiritual* history of America, and he hired the one man who knew where the bodies (and the tracts) were buried.

### **5.2 Retta Scott and the Worcester Dynasty**

**Retta Scott** (1916–1990) was the first woman to receive screen credit as an animator at Disney, famous for the vicious hunting dogs in *Bambi*.20

Genealogical research reveals a startling connection: Retta Scott married **Benjamin Fessenden Worcester**.15 The Worcester family was the **"Royal Family" of American Swedenborgianism**.

* **Rev. Thomas Worcester:** First pastor of the Boston Society of the New Jerusalem.  
* **John Worcester:** Prolific Swedenborgian theologian.  
* **Benjamin Worcester:** Retta's husband, deeply embedded in this lineage.

**Insight:** While Scott was hired before her marriage, her integration into the Worcester family placed her at the center of the studio’s Swedenborgian network. Her ability to animate "the inner beast" (the affection of the dogs) aligns with the Swedenborgian view that animals are correspondences of human affections/lusts. Walt’s promotion of Scott—against the skepticism of male animators—suggests he valued her unique "sight," a way of seeing the internal force of the animal that aligned with the studio's metaphysical goals.

### **5.3 Mary Blair and the Wayfarers Chapel**

**Mary Blair** (née Robinson), the stylist who defined the look of *Cinderella*, *Alice in Wonderland*, and *Peter Pan*, provides the strongest link to institutional Swedenborgianism.

* **Confirmation:** Mary Blair was confirmed into the Swedenborgian Church in 1974\.23  
* **Location:** She lived in Palos Verdes, home of the **Wayfarers Chapel** (The Glass Church).  
* **Architecture:** The Wayfarers Chapel was designed by **Lloyd Wright** (Frank Lloyd Wright’s son) as a National Memorial to Swedenborg.24 It is a masterpiece of "Organic Architecture," designed to blur the line between the indoors (spirit) and the outdoors (nature)—a physical manifestation of the Doctrine of Correspondences.

**Insight:** Mary Blair’s art—characterized by flatness, emotional color, and a rejection of Renaissance perspective—is a visual application of Swedenborgian aesthetics (seeing the spiritual *in* the natural, rather than the natural as a separate solid). Her eventual confirmation suggests that her artistic vision at Disney was an expression of a lifelong spiritual affinity, one that Walt Disney championed and protected against more realistic/materialistic animators. Walt didn't just like her art; he liked her *vision*, which was fundamentally correspondential.

**VI. The Frank Lloyd Wright Connection: Architecture of the Spirit**

The presence of **Frank Lloyd Wright** in the Disney orbit is another vector of Swedenborgian influence. Wright was deeply influenced by Swedenborgianism; his mother, Anna Lloyd Jones, was from a devout Unitarian/Swedenborgian family, and his mentor, Louis Sullivan, was a devotee of Swedenborg’s concept of "Form follows Function" (which is a secularized version of "Natural follows Spiritual").15

### **6.1 The 1939 Studio Visit: Validating the Abstract**

In 1939, Frank Lloyd Wright visited the Disney Studio and gave a lecture to the animators, a transcript of which survives.26 He brought a Russian animated film (*The Tale of the Czar Durandai*) and encouraged the Disney artists to pursue **abstract** and **organic** designs rather than strict realism.

Wright told them:

"Concern yourself with the best and finest thing... and do it to the top... You happen to have stumbled upon the future development of the cinema." 26

**Insight:** Wright’s validation was crucial. He was telling Walt that animation was the only medium capable of "Organic Art"—art where the form is perfectly responsive to the spirit. By inviting Wright, Walt was exposing his staff to the leading proponent of "Organic Architecture," a discipline rooted in the same Swedenborgian substrate as the "Organic Animation" Walt was building. This meeting likely emboldened Walt to push for the abstract sequences in *Fantasia*, which visualize sound (spirit) as color (form).

### **6.2 The Wayfarers Chapel as a Hub**

Decades later, Lloyd Wright (Frank’s son) built the **Wayfarers Chapel** in Palos Verdes.25 This chapel became a spiritual and social hub for the creative class in Los Angeles, including Disney artists like Mary Blair. It stands as a physical testament to the entanglement of the Disney aesthetic (nature, light, organic form) and Swedenborgian theology. Walt’s artists were getting married in a Swedenborgian temple designed by the son of the man who told Walt he was the future of art. The circle was closed.

**VII. The Library and the Landscape: Walt’s Personal Curations**

While a complete catalog of Walt Disney’s home library is not publicly available, the contents of his **office library** (cataloged by Dave Smith) and his known reading habits provide clues to his intellectual diet.29

### **7.1 The Office Library: A metaphysical spectrum**

Dave Smith’s catalog of Walt’s office includes over 600 books. While explicit theological tracts like *Arcana Coelestia* are absent (Walt was a pragmatist, not a theologian), the collection reveals a fascination with **New Thought** and **Metaphysical** literature, which are direct descendants of Swedenborgianism.

* **P.L. Travers:** Walt owned signed copies of *Mary Poppins* books.29 Travers was a student of Gurdjieff and deeply mystical. Her work deals with the "hidden worlds" inside the ordinary—a core Swedenborgian theme.  
* **L. Frank Baum:** *The Wizard of Oz*.29 Baum was a Theosophist, a movement heavily indebted to Swedenborg.  
* **Ernest Holmes and "Science of Mind":** Snippets suggest a connection to **Religious Science** (Science of Mind).30 Ernest Holmes, the founder, was heavily influenced by Swedenborg (via Emerson and Troward). The "Science of Mind" philosophy—that thoughts are things and the mind creates reality—is a modern psychological adaptation of the Doctrine of Influx.

**Insight:** Walt’s library shows he was reading the *secularized children* of Swedenborgianism (Theosophy, New Thought, Magical Realism) rather than the 18th-century father. He accessed the metaphysics through the cultural "digest" versions available in the 20th century.

### **7.2 Disneyland as a Swedenborgian Heaven**

Finally, Walt’s creation of **Disneyland** can be read as an act of Swedenborgian architecture. Swedenborg describes the "Heavenly City" not as a place of clouds, but as a place of **substantive reality** where the environment (buildings, gardens) changes instantly to reflect the "affections" of the inhabitants.

Disneyland is a "controlled environment" where "Form follows Spirit." Main Street USA is not a historical reconstruction; it is a "psychological construct" of safety and community. It is the "Celestial City" of Walt’s memory. By building Disneyland, Walt became the "God" of his own Swedenborgian universe, ensuring that every detail (natural effect) corresponded perfectly to the story (spiritual cause). He essentially built a Swedenborgian heaven on earth, where there is no decay, no dirt, and "the mind is the pilot" of the guest's experience.

**VIII. Conclusion: The Secular New Jerusalem**

The accumulated evidence leads to a definitive conclusion: Walt Disney’s involvement with Swedenborgianism was not a matter of formal religious conversion, but of **operational adoption**. He did not become a member of the Church of the New Jerusalem, but he built his studio and his theme park on its foundational physics.

1. **Intentionality:** The *Johnny Appleseed* production proves the studio consciously researched, processed, and encoded Swedenborgian theology. They knew what they were handling and chose to preserve its structure ("Blessings Three") while removing its label.  
2. **Pedagogy:** The "Action Analysis" training secularized the Doctrine of Influx ("Inner Force driving Outer Form"), turning a theological tenet into an industrial tool. "The mind is the pilot" became the studio’s First Commandment.  
3. **Personnel:** Walt actively hired and empowered "Carriers" of this tradition—Carl Carmer, Retta Scott, Mary Blair—who embedded the aesthetic and spiritual values of the New Church into the studio's DNA.  
4. **Personal Faith:** Walt’s "Deeds Rather Than Words" essay and his DeMolay background align with the Swedenborgian emphasis on *Use* over *Faith Alone*, creating a "Civil Religion" that was secretly powered by a Swedenborgian engine.

Walt Disney did not build a church; he built a studio. But in doing so, he created a **Secular New Jerusalem**—a "Kingdom of Uses" where art was not for art's sake, but for the sake of evoking "Love and Faith" in the audience. He solved the "Crisis of Ensoulment" by adopting the physics of the spirit. In the Disney cosmos, as in Swedenborg’s, the natural world is nothing but the mirror of the human heart.
